## Essentialist style

[Maxime's](https://maximevaillancourt.com/) website is peak essentialism. Focus only on what's _necessary_ and remove everything that doesn't contribute to the main purpose, which in this case is blogging.

Sadly, I can't implement his style (_I tried..._) because my site uses categories. I have [writings](/writings) and [projects](/projects) pages, which serve completely different purposes. I have non-blog pages like [now](/now), [about](/about), and [guestbook](/guestbook), which I can't display alongside blog pages. I have to put them in a different place, such as a menu.

This website is unique because it's _headerless_. Once you visit [Manu's](https://manuelmoreale.com/) homepage, you will be greeted by his latest blog post. After reading the post, you'll find navigation links underneath it, right after "**What should you do next?**". It's presented as a descriptive paragraph, which is a cool approach.

After that comes the beautiful archive list of posts. It works well if you write frequently, but if you don't (like me ðŸ˜…), you'll end up with gaps or just a month name with nothing under it.

## Card component idea

[Manu's](https://manuelmoreale.com/) navigation style reminds me of working with a Table of Contents in Microsoft Word. You have a heading with a page number on the right, and dots in the middle that guide the viewer's eye.

With that in mind, I created a `<Card>` component to show writings and projects page.
